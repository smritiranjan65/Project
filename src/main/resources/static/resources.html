Understanding Cyber Threats and Cyber Frauds

Types of Cyber Threats

Cyber threats are malicious attempts to damage or disrupt systems and networks. Here are some common types:

  • Malware: Malicious software designed to harm or exploit any programmable device, service, or network. This includes viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware.
  • Phishing: A technique where attackers send fraudulent communications, typically emails, that appear to come from a reputable source to steal sensitive data like login credentials or financial information.
  • Ransomware: A type of malware that encrypts the victim's files and demands a ransom payment to restore access to the data.
  • Denial of Service (DoS): An attack that aims to shut down a network or service by overwhelming it with traffic, rendering it inaccessible to legitimate users.
  • Man-in-the-Middle (MitM): An attack where the attacker secretly intercepts and potentially alters the communication between two parties without their knowledge.
  • SQL Injection: A code injection technique that exploits a vulnerability in an application's software by inserting malicious SQL statements into an input field, allowing attackers to interact with the database.

Types of Cyber Frauds

Cyber fraud involves deceptive practices that exploit the internet to deceive individuals or organizations for financial gain. Here are common types:

  • Online Scams: Deceptive schemes conducted over the internet to trick individuals into giving away money or personal information. Examples include lottery scams, fake charities, and get-rich-quick schemes.
  • Identity Theft: The fraudulent acquisition and use of someone else's personal information, typically for financial gain. This can occur through phishing, hacking, or stolen documents.
  • Credit Card Fraud: Unauthorized use of someone's credit card information to make purchases or withdraw funds. This can happen through phishing, card skimming, or data breaches.
  • Investment Fraud: Scams involving fraudulent investment opportunities that promise high returns with little risk. Examples include Ponzi schemes and fake investment platforms.
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC): A type of cyber fraud targeting businesses where attackers use email to impersonate executives or employees and trick others into transferring money or sensitive information.
  • Social Engineering: Manipulative tactics used to deceive individuals into divulging confidential information or performing actions that lead to security breaches. This can include pretexting, baiting, and tailgating.
